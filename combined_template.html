1word2vec: a name becomes a vector

Trained on ~100 billion words of Google News (2013). Each city name is a point in 300-dimensional space, learned purely from the words around it — names used in similar news contexts get similar vectors. No coordinates, no atlas: only text.

2cosine: the angle is the similarity

For every pair of cities we measure the angle between their two vectors: cos θ near 1 — used in the same contexts; near 0 — unrelated. All 7,626 pairs form the similarity matrix that both charts below read.

3MDS: the matrix becomes a map

Multidimensional scaling converts similarity to distance (d = 1 − cos) and searches for the 2-D layout whose point-to-point distances reproduce those dissimilarities as faithfully as possible (SMACOF; fit quality: stress-1 = 0.347).

Reading notes

  • Symmetry of the two views. The cutoff draws the same set of links on both charts, and hovering or clicking a city acts on both at once. On the geographic map a long link is a genuine cross-ocean bridge in news space; on the semantic map most links are short by construction — a long one means the projection had to compromise (semantically close, placed far apart).
  • Hover previews respect the cutoff: a city's top-5 neighbours draw solid where the pair clears the current cutoff and dashed where it does not, and the tooltip splits the list the same way. Click pins the city: the side panel ranks all 123 others (region glyph on every row), split at the cutoff, and all links draw on both charts — solid above, dashed and faint below. The slider moves the split live; Esc or × clears.
  • The cutoff defaults to 0.50 (≈450 of 7,626 pairs, average degree ≈ 7). Most links are short and regional — national constellations like the US, Indian, Chinese or Gulf cities; push the slider up to isolate the long-range bridges (e.g. the Gulf ↔ South Asia).
  • Orientation is real on both charts. The semantic layouts are rigidly fitted to true longitude/latitude: for MDS east–west genuinely tracks longitude (r 0.70), north–south barely tracks latitude (r 0.17) — word2vec encodes region and language far more than how far north a city sits.
  • Mexico City has no title-case token in the GoogleNews vocabulary (verified by a full scan); its vector is the mean of Mexico + City. The dateline token MEXICO_CITY was tried and rejected — its register-depressed similarities exile the city to the map edge.
  • St Petersburg uses Saint_Petersburg — the more frequent St._Petersburg is dominated by the Florida city (similarity to Tampa 0.68 vs Moscow 0.60).
  • Ho Chi Minh City maps to Saigon; Kyiv resolves to its own token in this 2013-era corpus.
  • Lisbon drifts away from Iberia — its vector is polluted by Treaty-of-Lisbon news coverage. Corpus artifacts like this are part of what the map reveals.

Methodology

  1. Embedding model. GoogleNews-vectors-negative300: word2vec (skip-gram with negative sampling) trained by Google on ~100 billion words of Google News text (circa 2013). Vocabulary of 3 million tokens, 300 dimensions per vector, with frequent multiword phrases merged into single tokens (New_York, Buenos_Aires). Under the distributional hypothesis, cities mentioned in similar news contexts receive similar vectors — no geographic information enters the similarity computation at any point.
  2. City list. 124 cities curated by hand: metro population roughly >1 million and global prominence, deliberately balanced across 9 regions rather than ranked by population — a strict top-N by population would be dominated by Chinese and South-Asian metros, and the map would lose most of the regions a general reader recognizes. Region labels are used only for coloring — they play no role in the computation.
  3. Token resolution. Each city has an ordered list of candidate tokens (explicit overrides first, then the underscored display name and a diacritics-stripped variant). A single streaming pass over the 1.74 GB binary matches candidates byte-exactly against all 3M vocabulary entries; the first candidate present wins. Absences are verified, not assumed: Mexico_City, New_York_City, New_Delhi and Ho_Chi_Minh_City genuinely do not exist in this vocabulary. Ambiguous tokens were tested empirically before choosing (see reading notes: the Tampa test for St Petersburg, the dateline-register test for Mexico City).
  4. Similarity matrix. Vectors are L2-normalized and all pairwise cosine similarities computed: cos(u,v) = u·v / (‖u‖‖v‖), giving a symmetric 124×124 matrix (7,626 unique pairs). Observed range −0.06 (Houston–Lisbon) to 0.86 (Sydney–Melbourne), mean 0.30.
  5. Dissimilarity: why d = 1 − cos. Three monotone transforms were compared empirically on this data with identical SMACOF runs (monotone ⇒ a nonmetric MDS would not distinguish them at all):

    The textbook objection to 1 − cos is that it is only a semi-metric (it can violate the triangle inequality, while the chord √(2(1−cos)) and the angle arccos are true metrics). On this dataset the objection is empirically empty: 0 violations in 200,000 sampled triples (similarities only reach 0.86, far from the regime where violations appear). Meanwhile 1 − cos fits its target best, preserves the cosine ranking best, and the three layouts are nearly identical anyway (Procrustes disparity ≤ 0.005). Chord would be the safer default for datasets with near-duplicate items; here the simplest transform is also the best-behaved, so the map keeps d = 1 − cos.
  6. MDS. Metric MDS via SMACOF (scikit-learn 1.9) on the precomputed dissimilarities: 2 components, classical-MDS (Torgerson) initialization followed by stress-majorization iterations (max 3000, tolerance 10−9), which makes the solution deterministic. SMACOF minimizes raw stress Σ (d̂ij − dij)² between input dissimilarities d̂ and embedding distances d.
  7. Fit diagnostics. Kruskal stress-1 = √(Σ(d̂ij − dij)² / Σ dij²) = 0.347; Pearson correlation between input dissimilarities and 2-D distances r = 0.71 (Spearman ρ = 0.71). By Kruskal's rule of thumb (<0.05 excellent, >0.2 poor) this is a heavily compressed fit — unavoidable when flattening the high-rank similarity structure of 300-d embeddings into a plane. Read the map accordingly: cluster membership and adjacency are trustworthy; small distance differences are not. The hover overlay shows each city's true 300-d nearest neighbours precisely so the projection's compromises stay visible.
  8. Alternative layouts (the switcher). UMAP and t-SNE answer a different question than MDS. Both run on the same vectors with cosine distance: UMAP (n_neighbors 15, min_dist 0.3) builds a nearest-neighbour graph and lays the graph out; t-SNE (perplexity 20) matches neighbour probabilities. They preserve neighbourhoods far better than MDS — recall@10 rises from 0.52 to 0.70–0.71 — at the price that distances between clusters stop meaning anything, which is why MDS (the only distance-faithful method) stays the default view of this demo. All three layouts are aligned to geography by the same rigid Procrustes step, so the east–west reading survives switching.
  9. Orientation (geographic). An MDS solution is unique only up to rotation, reflection and translation, so the raw axes are meaningless. Instead of an arbitrary convention, the configuration is aligned to real geography by orthogonal Procrustes: the rotation + reflection that best maps the embedding onto each city's true (longitude, latitude), with the two geographic axes standardized so east–west and north–south count equally. This is a rigid transform — it preserves every pairwise distance and the stress exactly, and only decides which way is up. The result: east–west correspondence with longitude jumps from an arbitrary r 0.48 to 0.70, and north–south flips right-side-up (r −0.21 → +0.17). Latitude stays weak because even the single best rotation cannot push it past r 0.21 — the semantic structure simply does not encode north–south.
  10. Geographic network view. The same similarity matrix drawn over real geography: cities at their true (longitude, latitude) on an equirectangular projection, with a simplified Natural Earth 110m land outline embedded in the page (no external tiles). A link joins every pair with cosine ≥ the cutoff; width and opacity grow with similarity. The slider spans 0.30–0.86; the default 0.50 keeps ≈450 of 7,626 pairs (edge count vs cutoff: 0.45 → 740, 0.50 → 451, 0.55 → 265, 0.60 → 164, 0.70 → 64).
  11. Beyond word2vec. The 2013 model is kept deliberately — it is the canonical teaching model, ships ready-made phrase tokens, and its quirks (Lisbon, St Petersburg) are instructive. Better representations exist: Wikipedia2Vec embeds Wikipedia entities, so "Santiago (Chile)" and "Santiago de Cuba" stop sharing one vector — the cleanest fix for name collisions; fastText adds subword information and handles unseen spellings; GloVe is a close word2vec alternative trained on global co-occurrence counts; modern sentence/LLM embeddings could encode a whole city description, trading the elegant "meaning from raw co-occurrence" story for quality. Swapping the embedding layer changes step 1 only — the rest of this pipeline (cosine → dissimilarity → layout) stays identical.
  12. Known limitations. The corpus is 2013-era news: prominence reflects that period's coverage (Kyiv still resolves, but from pre-2014 usage). A token conflates every sense of the string — Treaty-of-Lisbon coverage displaces Lisbon, Santiago and Hyderabad name multiple cities, and news salience, not geography, drives similarity. One vector (Mexico City) is a two-token composition rather than a native token. Map links are straight lines, not great-circle arcs, and the equirectangular projection inflates high latitudes.
