124 world cities by word2vec similarity

a teaching demo by Ivan Pozdniakov

“You shall know a word by the company it keeps.”— J. R. Firth (1957)

Could a computer sketch a map of the world having read the news, but never seen an atlas? Every city name here is turned into an embedding — a vector of 300 numbers learned by word2vec from the contexts the name appears in, so that similar usage means similar numbers (how: cards 1–4 below). Comparing all vectors gives a 124×124 similarity matrix, shown in two views. Geographic network: cities at their real coordinates, links join pairs above the similarity cutoff. Semantic map: position itself encodes similarity — the layout is computed from the matrix alone. Hover a city for its top-5 neighbours; click to pin it and see the full ranked list.

124 cities · 9 regions · 7,626 pairs · cosine range −.06 to .86 · MDS Stress-1 = .347

1word2vec: meaning from context

An embedding represents a word as numbers, chosen so that similar usage gives similar numbers. word2vec learns them by reading ~100 billion words of Google News (2013) and training a small neural network to predict each word from the words around it (Mikolov et al., 2013). Names that occur in interchangeable contexts end up with nearly the same numbers:
…storms lashed Sydney late on Friday…
…storms lashed Melbourne late on Friday…
…the mayor of New York unveiled the budget…
Shared contexts → similar vectors; different contexts → different vectors. All three cities are English-speaking — what separates them is whose news they share, not language. No atlas, no coordinates: the model never sees geography, only text.

3cosine: the angle between vectors

The same list of numbers can be drawn as an arrow: 300 numbers = an arrow in a 300-axis space (two axes shown). The closer two arrows point the same way, the smaller the angle θ between them, and cos θ turns that angle into a similarity score: cos 0° = 1 for identical directions, cos 90° = 0 for unrelated ones. 4MDS: a map from distances alone

From a table of road distances between cities you could redraw the map of a country. MDS (Kruskal, 1964) does exactly that — but the distances are semantic, d = 1 − cos θ, not physical: it finds the 2-D points whose mutual distances best reproduce the whole 124×124 table (fit: Stress-1 = .347). UMAP and t-SNE (switcher below) answer the same question differently — they skip the global distance table and build the map straight from each vector's nearest neighbours.

Here position is real geography — every city sits at its true coordinates. The word2vec similarity lives only in the lines: a link joins each pair above the cutoff, and thicker = more similar. Read it as a test of Tobler's first law of geography — "everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related than distant things" (Tobler, 1970) — against news text: mostly the law wins, and the exceptions are the interesting part.

Methodology

  1. Embedding model. GoogleNews-vectors-negative300: word2vec (skip-gram with negative sampling) trained by Google on ~100 billion words of Google News text, circa 2013 (Mikolov et al., 2013). Vocabulary of 3 million tokens, 300 dimensions per vector, with frequent multiword phrases merged into single tokens (New_York, Buenos_Aires). Under the distributional hypothesis (Harris, 1954), cities mentioned in similar news contexts receive similar vectors — no geographic information enters the similarity computation at any point. Two caveats follow directly from the training data: what drives similarity is news salience, not geography (a shared war, league or stock exchange pulls cities together as surely as a shared border), and the corpus is frozen in ~2013 — prominence and usage reflect that period's coverage (Kyiv already resolves to its own token, but from pre-2014 usage).
  2. City list. 124 cities curated by hand: metro population roughly >1 million and global prominence, deliberately balanced across 9 regions rather than ranked by population — a strict top-N by population would be dominated by Chinese and South-Asian metros, and the map would lose most of the regions a general reader recognizes. Region labels are used only for coloring — they play no role in the computation.
  3. Token resolution. word2vec has no notion of "city" — only of tokens, so each city must be mapped to the vocabulary entry that best carries its meaning. Multiword names are the tricky part: GoogleNews merged word pairs into single tokens automatically, keeping only combinations that co-occur unusually often, so coverage is arbitrary — New_York and Buenos_Aires exist, while Mexico_City, New_York_City, New_Delhi and Ho_Chi_Minh_City genuinely do not (verified by scanning all 3M entries; the resolver tries an ordered candidate list and takes the first hit). The decisions that needed judgment:
    • Mexico City has no usable token at all, so its vector is the average of Mexico and City — the standard fallback for a missing phrase. An alternative existed: MEXICO_CITY, an all-caps token born from agency datelines ("MEXICO CITY (Reuters) —…"). It was tested and rejected: dateline tokens live among other datelines, so their cosines to ordinary words run uniformly low, and the city was exiled to the edge of every map.
    • Ho Chi Minh City lost its full name to the same phrase lottery (only fragments like Minh_City survive), but 2013 news text overwhelmingly calls the city Saigon — a well-trained single token with the right meaning, so it is used instead.
    • St Petersburg is an ambiguity case: the frequent St._Petersburg is dominated by St. Petersburg, Florida (cos to Tampa = .68 vs Moscow = .60), so the map uses Saint_Petersburg, which is cleanly Russian.
    • New York and Delhi simply fall back to New_York and Delhi; Kyiv resolves to its own token even in this era.
    The unavoidable residue: a token fuses every sense of its string. Treaty-of-Lisbon coverage drags Lisbon away from Iberia, Santiago and Hyderabad each name several cities, and no resolver can untangle that — entity-level embeddings can (step 12).
  4. Similarity matrix. Vectors are L2-normalized and all pairwise cosine similarities computed, cos(u,v) = u·v / (‖u‖ ‖v‖) giving a symmetric 124×124 matrix (7,626 unique pairs). Observed range −.06 (Houston–Lisbon) to .86 (Sydney–Melbourne), M = .30.
  5. Dissimilarity: why d = 1 − cos θ. Three monotone transforms were compared empirically on this data with identical SMACOF runs (monotone ⇒ a nonmetric MDS would not distinguish them at all):

    The textbook objection to 1 − cos is that it is only a semi-metric: it can violate the triangle inequality, while the chord √(2(1−cos)) and the angle arccos are true metrics (Borg & Groenen, 2005). On this dataset the objection is empirically empty: 0 violations in 200,000 sampled triples (similarities only reach .86, far from the regime where violations appear). Meanwhile 1 − cos fits its target best, preserves the cosine ranking best, and the three layouts are nearly identical anyway (Procrustes disparity ≤ .005). Chord would be the safer default for datasets with near-duplicate items; here the simplest transform is also the best-behaved, so the map keeps d = 1 − cos.
  6. MDS. Metric MDS (Kruskal, 1964; Borg & Groenen, 2005) via SMACOF (scikit-learn 1.9) on the precomputed dissimilarities: 2 components, classical-MDS (Torgerson) initialization followed by stress-majorization iterations (max 3000, tolerance 10−9), which makes the solution deterministic. SMACOF minimizes raw stress Σ(d̂ₖₗ − dₖₗ)² between input dissimilarities d̂ and embedding distances d.
  7. Fit diagnostics. Kruskal Stress-1, Stress-1 = √(Σ(d̂ − d)² / Σ d²) = .347 with Pearson correlation between input dissimilarities and 2-D distances r = .71 (Spearman ρ = .71). By Kruskal's rule of thumb (<.05 excellent, >.20 poor) this is a heavily compressed fit — unavoidable when flattening the high-rank similarity structure of 300-d embeddings into a plane. Read the map accordingly: cluster membership and adjacency are trustworthy; small distance differences are not. The hover overlay shows each city's true 300-d nearest neighbours precisely so the projection's compromises stay visible.
  8. Alternative layouts (the switcher). The four algorithms, in historical order, answer progressively different questions. PCA (Pearson, 1901) projects the cloud onto the two directions of greatest variance — linear, so it cannot bend around curved structure and keeps the fewest true neighbourhoods (recall@10 = .41). MDS optimizes distances globally (Stress-1 = .347, recall@10 = .52) and is the only distance-faithful view, hence the default. t-SNE (van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008) and UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018) are nonlinear neighbourhood methods on the same vectors with cosine distance (perplexity = 20; n_neighbors = 15, min_dist = 0.3): they preserve local structure far better (recall@10 = .71 / .70) at the price that distances between clusters stop meaning anything. All four layouts are aligned to geography by the same rigid Procrustes step, so the east–west reading survives switching; the metric definitions sit under the switcher itself.
  9. Orientation (geographic). An MDS solution is unique only up to rotation, reflection and translation, so the raw axes are meaningless. Instead of an arbitrary convention, the configuration is aligned to real geography by orthogonal Procrustes: the rotation + reflection that best maps the embedding onto each city's true (longitude, latitude), with the two geographic axes standardized so east–west and north–south count equally. This is a rigid transform — it preserves every pairwise distance and the stress exactly, and only decides which way is up. The result: east–west correspondence with longitude jumps from an arbitrary r = .48 to r = .70, and north–south flips right-side-up (r = −.21 → +.17). Latitude stays weak because even the single best rotation cannot push it past r ≈ .21 — the semantic structure simply does not encode north–south.
  10. Geographic network view. The same similarity matrix drawn over real geography: cities at their true (longitude, latitude) on an equirectangular projection, with a simplified Natural Earth 110m land outline embedded in the page (no external tiles). A link joins every pair with cosine ≥ the cutoff; width and opacity grow with similarity. The slider spans 0.30–0.86; the default 0.50 keeps ≈450 of 7,626 pairs (edge count vs cutoff: 0.45 → 740, 0.50 → 451, 0.55 → 265, 0.60 → 164, 0.70 → 64).
  11. Clustered similarity matrix. The heatmap section shows the full 124×124 matrix with rows and columns reordered by agglomerative hierarchical clustering: average linkage on d = 1 − cos θ with optimal leaf ordering (SciPy). No cluster count is chosen — the dendrogram records the entire merge history, and the reordering alone makes block structure visible. It is the exact matrix from card 4, complete and seriated: diagonal blocks = regions, off-block warm bands = cross-region bridges (find the Gulf ↔ South Asia patch), pale rows = cities similar to nothing in particular.
  12. Beyond word2vec. The 2013 model is kept deliberately — it is the canonical teaching model, ships ready-made phrase tokens, and its quirks (Lisbon, St Petersburg) are instructive. Better representations exist: Wikipedia2Vec embeds Wikipedia entities, so "Santiago (Chile)" and "Santiago de Cuba" stop sharing one vector — the cleanest fix for name collisions (Yamada et al., 2020); fastText adds subword information and handles unseen spellings (Bojanowski, Grave, Joulin, & Mikolov, 2017); GloVe is a close word2vec alternative trained on global co-occurrence counts (Pennington, Socher, & Manning, 2014); modern sentence/LLM embeddings could encode a whole city description, trading the elegant "meaning from raw co-occurrence" story for quality. Swapping the embedding layer changes step 1 only — the rest of this pipeline (cosine → dissimilarity → layout) stays identical.
